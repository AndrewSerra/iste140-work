Incentives in Technology

In our day-to-day lives, we don’t really observe all the details around use that an engineer or designer may have planted. Think of a microwave, it’s just a tool to heat up a meal right? Not so much. Think of the effort and research that may go into how each button is added or whether it should even be there.

Over decades companies collect data on how these products are used. Then the devices go through their own evolution process. We design products to have less cognitive complexity during their usage process. This would be a much more critical example but imagine this: A pilot is flying the plane you are a passenger in. The instrumentation that they have to check is spread out on the dashboard. They keep moving their eyes from one end to the other, and it keeps going on for the duration of the flight.

How can this be fixed? Operational complexity can be decreased by rearranging the instrumentation on the dashboard. An approach is to group tools that are checked one after the other in certain cases. One example of this is from research conducted by Manooch S. Saeedi at Western Kentucky University. The research focused on optimal cognitive load and motivation to solve math problems. Their findings in their first experiment showed that with a decrease in cognitive load and an increase in intrinsic motivation, there was an increase in performance.[6] This can apply to the user's performance on completing a task using a tool.

What will be explained in this website is how we are intrinsically motivated to interact with technology, and perform well to complete the actions we pursue. Continue to the page, "The Story", to see topics that are frequently discussed in the industry.
